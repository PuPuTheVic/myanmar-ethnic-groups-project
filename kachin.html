Kachin

The Kachin people live mainly in northern Myanmar and are known for their colorful traditions and unity festivals.

Basic Info

KACHIN (also known as Jinghpaw)
  • Subgroups: Jinghpaw, Rawang, Lisu, Zaiwa, Lawngwaw(Maru), Lachid, Hpun(Nung)
  • Population: Approximately 1.6–1.7 million
  • Languages Spoken: Jinghpaw (lingua franca), plus other subgroup languages; many also speak Burmese and English

History & Background

The Kachin people are believed to have migrated from the Tibetan plateau into northern Myanmar centuries ago. They settled in the mountainous regions of Kachin State, maintaining clan-based societies. Historically, Kachin groups practiced shifting cultivation and maintained semi-autonomous governance.

During British colonial rule, the Kachin Hills were recognized as a frontier area. Kachin soldiers played a significant role in World War II, fighting alongside the Allies in the Burma Campaign.

Important historical figures include Duwa Zan Hta Sin (traditional chief), and General Gun Maw (Kachin Independence Army). The Kachin Independence Organization (KIO) has been central in the community’s modern struggle for autonomy since 1961.

Geography & Location

  • Main region: Kachin State, northern Myanmar (bordering China and India)
  • Major towns: Myitkyina (capital), Bhamo, Putao, Hpakant
  • Tourist attractions: Indawgyi Lake (largest inland lake in Myanmar), Putao (gateway to snow-capped mountains), jade mines in Hpakant

Culture & Traditions

  • Traditional Dress: Kachin women often wear bright jackets, striped skirts, and silver jewelry. Men wear traditional tunics, turbans, and swords (dah). Each subgroup has distinct designs. Each design reflects clan heritage and is proudly worn at ceremonies and festivals.
  • Music & Dance: The Manau dance is the most famous, performed during the Manau Festival. It symbolizes unity and prosperity. Traditional instruments include drums, gongs, and flutes.
  • Festivals: The Kachin Manau Festival (January) is the largest cultural celebration, featuring ritual dances around tall decorated posts. It celebrates unity, harvest, and historical victories.
  • Cuisines: Kachin cuisine includes rice, pork, beef, freshwater fish, wild herbs, and dishes like 'Shat Jam' (mixed rice).
  • Arts & Crafts: Skilled in weaving, sword-making, and silverwork. Traditional designs often feature symbolic patterns representing nature and clan identity.

Religion & Beliefs

Christianity is the dominant religion among Kachins today, introduced by American Baptist missionaries in the late 19th century. A large portion of the population is Baptist or Catholic.

Traditional beliefs (animism) are still practiced, including worship of spirits (nats) and rituals for prosperity, fertility, and protection. Some practices blend with Christian faith.

Economy & Lifestyle

  • Main livelihoods: farming (rice, corn, tea, opium historically), jade mining (Hpakant is world-famous for jade), logging, and cross-border trade with China.
  • Housing: Traditional Kachin houses are made of wood and bamboo, often raised on stilts, with thatched or tin roofs.
  • Role in Myanmar’s economy: Kachin jade and timber are major exports; also significant contributors to agriculture and energy (hydropower dams).

Notable Heritage & Contributions

  • The Manau Festival is one of Myanmar’s most important cultural celebrations.
  • Kachin soldiers contributed significantly during World War II in the fight against Japanese forces.
  • Famous figures include Brang Seng (former KIO chairman), Ludu Daw Amar (writer of Kachin descent), and contemporary leaders of the peace process.
